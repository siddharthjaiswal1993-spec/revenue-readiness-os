# Sample Evaluation Rubrics

---

## Rubric 1: Discovery Call

**Scenario type:** Discovery  
**Key skills assessed:** Discovery, Value Articulation, Executive Communication

| Score | Level | Description |
|-------|-------|-------------|
| 85–100 | Excellent | Rep systematically uncovers current state, desired state, and cost of inaction before any solution discussion. Probes root causes, not just symptoms. Asks open-ended questions throughout. Summarizes understanding back to buyer to confirm. Does not propose solution until problem scope is clear. |
| 65–84 | Proficient | Rep covers the essential discovery areas. May jump to one product mention but redirects back to questions. Buyer understands the rep is listening. Gaps in probing for business impact. |
| 45–64 | Developing | Rep asks questions but mix of open and closed. Moves to solution talk too early (before establishing problem scope). May miss key discovery areas (timeline, decision process, current solution). |
| 25–44 | At Risk | Rep leads with product features or benefits. Discovery questions are shallow or scripted. Does not adapt to buyer responses. Buyer feels talked at. |
| 0–24 | Critical | Rep skips discovery entirely or conducts a box-checking exercise. No curiosity evident. Buyer disengaged within the first few turns. |

**Scoring signals:**
- Q-rate (questions per turn): excellent ≥ 1.2; developing ≤ 0.6
- First solution mention: excellent at turn 8+; critical at turn 1–2
- Current state confirmed before proposing: yes/no binary
- Buyer response length trend: increasing (rep is earning trust) vs. decreasing (buyer disengaging)

## Rubric 2: Pricing Objection Handling

**Scenario type:** Objection — Pricing  
**Key skills assessed:** Objection Handling, Value Articulation, Closing Discipline

| Score | Level | Description |
|-------|-------|-------------|
| 85–100 | Excellent | Rep acknowledges the objection without deflecting. Investigates root cause ("is it absolute budget or relative to expected ROI?"). Ties value back to previously established pain with specific dollar framing. Offers a path forward (phased implementation, ROI guarantee discussion, different tier). Does not discount without concession. |
| 65–84 | Proficient | Rep acknowledges and addresses objection. Value reframe is present but generic (not tied to buyer's specific pain). Path forward is offered. May be slightly defensive on pricing. |
| 45–64 | Developing | Rep gets defensive or apologetic about price. Jumps to discount offer without probing. Value argument is present but disconnected from buyer's stated pain. |
| 25–44 | At Risk | Rep offers discounts immediately without understanding what the objection is really about. Value argument absent or generic. Does not ask about next steps. |
| 0–24 | Critical | Rep capitulates entirely to price pressure, agrees the product might be too expensive, or loses composure. No attempt to reframe value. |

**Red flags:**
- "I understand, we are expensive" (without reframe) → -10 points
- Discount offered in first response to pricing objection → -15 points
- No attempt to understand root cause of objection → -10 points

## Rubric 3: Competitive Objection

**Scenario type:** Objection — Competitive  
**Key skills assessed:** Competitive Positioning, Objection Handling

| Score | Level | Description |
|-------|-------|-------------|
| 85–100 | Excellent | Rep acknowledges competitor without disparaging them. Anchors response to buyer's specific context ("based on what you told me about your situation..."). Articulates 2–3 concrete differentiated capabilities relevant to that context. Does not claim features the product doesn't have. Redirects to a proof point (reference customer, trial). |
| 65–84 | Proficient | Rep handles objection without getting defensive. Differentiators stated but may not be connected to buyer's specific pain. May use a generic positioning statement. |
| 45–64 | Developing | Rep gives a generic differentiation answer. Does not connect to buyer's context. May bad-mouth competitor or make vague capability claims. |
| 25–44 | At Risk | Rep is defensive or dismissive. Competitive claims are vague or unsubstantiated. Does not offer any proof point or next step. |
| 0–24 | Critical | Rep disparages competitor, makes false claims, or is unable to provide any differentiation. Loses buyer trust. |

## Rubric 4: Product Demo Storytelling

**Scenario type:** Demo  
**Key skills assessed:** Demo Storytelling, Product Knowledge, Value Articulation

| Score | Level | Description |
|-------|-------|-------------|
| 85–100 | Excellent | Demo is structured as a buyer journey, not a feature tour. Rep connects each feature demonstration to a specific pain the buyer mentioned in discovery. Uses buyer's language. Checks for engagement throughout. Demo is paced for the buyer — slows down on relevant capabilities, skips irrelevant ones. Ends with a clear value summary. |
| 65–84 | Proficient | Demo references discovery findings. Pacing is reasonable. May linger on features that were not relevant to buyer's stated pain. Buyer is engaged. |
| 45–64 | Developing | Demo follows standard script. Limited customization to buyer context. Feature-focused rather than outcome-focused. Buyer may look disengaged. |
| 25–44 | At Risk | Feature tour with no narrative thread. Rep is reading from a script. No connection to buyer's stated problems. Buyer checks out. |
| 0–24 | Critical | Rep is unfamiliar with the product or confused about features. Demo crashes (metaphorically). Buyer has no understanding of what problem this solves. |

## Rubric 5: Executive Communication

**Scenario type:** Executive Pitch  
**Key skills assessed:** Executive Communication, Value Articulation, Closing Discipline

| Score | Level | Description |
|-------|-------|-------------|
| 85–100 | Excellent | Opens with business outcome (not product name). Communicates in the executive's language: revenue, risk, time to value, strategic fit. Concise — no rambling. Listens actively and adjusts based on executive's direction. Makes a specific ask at the end. Does not spend time on feature details unprompted. |
| 65–84 | Proficient | Good business framing. May spend slightly too much time on product. Ask is present but not specific enough. |
| 45–64 | Developing | Starts with product overview. ROI language is generic ("save time and money"). No specific next step. May be too long for an executive interaction. |
| 25–44 | At Risk | Feature-focused conversation at an executive level. No business framing. Executive becomes impatient. No clear ask. |
| 0–24 | Critical | Rep is not prepared for an executive audience. Unprepared for questions. No ask. Executive terminates the conversation. |

## Rubric 6: Security Review Handling

**Scenario type:** Technical Objection — Security  
**Key skills assessed:** Security Review Handling, Product Knowledge, Objection Handling

| Score | Level | Description |
|-------|-------|-------------|
| 85–100 | Excellent | Rep is confident, not defensive. Can speak to security architecture at a high level without pretending to be a security engineer. Knows when to bring in a solution engineer or security specialist. Has answers to common questions (data residency, encryption, certifications, access controls). Validates customer's security requirements as legitimate and takes them seriously. |
| 65–84 | Proficient | Good composure. Handles most questions well. May have one gap and knows to escalate appropriately. |
| 45–64 | Developing | Becomes defensive or uncertain on security questions. May deflect without committing to a follow-up. Basic answers but lacks depth. |
| 25–44 | At Risk | Unable to answer basic security questions. Becomes visibly uncomfortable. Does not know how to bring in the right resources. |
| 0–24 | Critical | Security conversation goes sideways. Rep makes incorrect statements, dismisses the objection, or cannot hold the conversation at all. Deal at risk. |
